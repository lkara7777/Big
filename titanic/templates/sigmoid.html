Product's life cycle - Sigmoid curve


Some data take the form of an elongated S when plotted and the curve has the following characteristics:
- very small initial slope,
- followed by a period of increasing slope which gives way to constant slope
- and decreasing slope approaches zero.


Pearl equation can be used to fit S shaped data. The fitting problem is called Sigmoid Regression.
Often when plotted over time the sales of a product will follow an S-shaped curve. Fitting an S-shaped curve to the first few months of data is important
because it enables us to estimate how long it will take for sales to level off.
It is critical to find the point where the S-shaped curve starts to flatten out so as companies to find a second curve by launching another product.




Real example:

We gather the sales's quantity of a product A for the period: 06/2017 - 04/2020. And we predict the sales's quantity of the product with the sigmoid function.
Blue line is consisted from real data for every month and red line is constructed from the following function that predicted the sales'quantity:

y=75800/(1+ 404.4*exp(-0.306*t))   [1920 by Raymond Pearl]


Apparently, it is doing a very good job predicting the monthly sales' quantity. Considering the rate of change in sales'quantity,
product A has already reached the point of flatten out.Thus,decisions should have been taken to strengthen the product A or launch similar product.


On a practical level, that means that we need to take a good, hard look at the status of the activities that sustain us. We have to evaluate where both our business and technology lie on the curve. It would be obvious to all when a company is in decline.
The answer is pretty simple, actually – you seek out and launch your next path to success while you’re still basking in the glow of the present moment’s achievement. Basically, you’re adding a second sigmoid onto your first.
